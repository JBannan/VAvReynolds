Background & Scenario

Background

The company CauliflowerOnline provides an email service with thousands of registered users. They are able to provide high quality service to their users across the east coast due to their server hubs that handle email transmission for specified regions. One of these hubs is located in Fairfax, Virginia. Jeremy Reynolds is a member of GoodIntentions of Greater Washington, a non-profit organization known for retail stores across the D.C. metropolitan area. GoodIntentions uses the funds received from their retail sales and purchases of merchandise to further their mission to help impoverished families in the D.C. metro area.

Scenario

Jeremy Reynolds obtained an email list of users registered with CauliflowerOnline and other email service providers and sent over 12,000 unsolicited bulk emails to these users on 4 separate occasions advertising the charity’s mission and retail locations. The emails presented an overview of GoodIntentions’ history and how they achieve their mission. The emails also suggested purchasing merchandise, donating items to the stores, and shopping at the stores to support the charity and its mission. With this sudden rush of emails, CauliflowerOnline noticed a brief spike in activity being placed on their servers.

Questions Presented

Legal Question

Is the defendant legally liable for SPAM under Virginia or Federal Law?

Supporting Case:

XMission, L.C. v. Click Sales, Inc., 2019 U.S. Dist.

  1. This case revolved around alleged violations of 15 USCS § 7701 of the CAN-SPAM Act, as XMission, an ISP, alleged irreparable harm to its reputation based on slowed speeds as a result of 116,000 commercial emails sent promoting Click Sales products.
  2. This case corresponds to the quantity of SPAM as well as the adverse effect legal elements, as they proved the amount of spam passing through their servers caused traffic to slow and (long-term), a loss of customers.
  3. The case ruled in XMission’s favor, thus, helps the plaintiff if they prove an adverse effect as a result of bulk emails transmitting through their servers.

Opposing Case:

Seidl v. Greentree Mortg. Co., F. Supp. 2d 1292
  1. Concerns revenue from SPAM legal element; if revenue from SPAM is generated at the expense of the plaintiff, it is grounds for a lawsuit. The plaintiff was a student suing a mortgage company (the defendant) for sending unsolicited bulk emails; he then spoofed a company email address and sent thousands of emails of a nonprofit himself. The mortgage company countersued alleging racketeering.

Ethical Question:

Is it ethical for a non-profit organization to send emails soliciting money from sales transactions if for a non-commercial purpose?

Media Research:

Fritz, Joanne. “What Nonprofits Must Do to Comply With the CAN-SPAM Act.” The Balance Small Business Journal, The Balance Small Business Journal, 14 Dec. 2018, www.thebalancesmb.com/what-nonprofits-must-do-to-comply-with-the-can-spam-act-2502412.

Article outlining the federal guidelines that all commercial companies sending promotional emails must comply with; since there is no exemption for non-profit organizations (like GoodIntentions), they also are beholden to these guidelines.

Paynter, Ben. “Charities Are Spamming You Because It Works.” Fast Company, Fast Company, 10 Aug. 2018, www.fastcompany.com/90216187/charities-are-spamming-you-because-it-works.

Article on a study conducted by a charity consultancy on the success (open) rate of charity solicitation emails, and why non-profit organizations are increasing the amount of emails they send to mailing lists (the study shows increasing monetary returns based on the responses to the survey, particularly among young adults).

Legal Elements

To hold the defendant liable under Virginia law, the plaintiff must prove:

  1. Reynolds used a computer or computer network located in Virginia in order to send the emails to CauliflowerOnline’s users. If Reynolds used a computer outside of the state, the emails must have been transmitted through CauliflowerOnline’s servers in Fairfax.

To hold the hold the defendant liable for spam, the plaintiff must prove one of the following under Va. Code Ann. Section 18.2-152.3:1:

  1. The volume of spam transmitted exceeded 10,000 attempted recipients in a 24 hour period, 100,000 attempted recipients in any 30-day time period, or one million attempted recipients in any one-year time period.
  2. The revenue generated from a specific spam exceeded $1,000 or the total revenue generated from all spam transmitted to any EMSP exceeded $ 50,000.

Or the following under the CAN-SPAM Act:

  1. The defendant caused an adverse effect or significant, presentable burden on email recipients, i.e. bandwidth, network connectivity, signal strength, as a result of frequently sent emails. If no opt-out or unsubscribe options are clearly presented to recipients, a presentable burden on their inbox or spam filters would suffice.

Detailed Witness Statements

Plaintiff Witness: Redford Herring, Attorney General of the Commonwealth of Virginia

The Honourable Redford Herring, Attorney General of the Commonwealth of Virginia, is the acting power of attorney for the state government, and is seeking to prosecute the defendant on violations of the ‘TUBES’ (transmission of unsolicited bulk electronic mails) statute of the Virginia Computer Crimes Act.

Long before his election as a public official for the state government, he worked his way up after graduating with honors from the UVA School of Law by taking small clerking positions here and there, and then working as associate corporate counsel for several medium-sized companies, one of which happened to be CauliflowerOnline Corporation (then known by its founding name, Broccol-eMail, Inc.); this tenure lasted from 1996-2002. He continued in corporate law for a brief time after this before establishing his own practice, Red Herring Law Firm, P.C. in 2004, being the principal associate until he ventured into public office by being elected chief deputy attorney general by voters in a special election in 2010; he has been re-elected twice since then (in 2012 and 2016, respectively).

Pro-Plaintiff Knowledge:

Having a storied history as a law practitioner, Mr. Herring is knowledgeable regarding the state’s anti-spam laws; he interjected himself into the case on behalf of the state police whose cybercrimes division wanted to go after the defendant (Mr. Reynolds) after tracing the bulk emails back to him.

Pro-Defendant Knowledge:

Due to his publicized history with the CauliflowerOnline organization (albeit under its former name), he could present a potential conflict of interest that the defense could assert as justification for having the case thrown out.


Plaintiff Witness: CauliflowerOnline Representative

CauliflowerOnline is an email service provider with hundreds of thousands of users. The company was founded with the mission of giving users an efficient communication platform. CauliflowerOnline has always operated with the goal of providing uninterrupted service to its users; for this reason, it has kept its user base relatively small, at least as far as email service providers go.

In its eight years of operation, CauliflowerOnline has served its users well, carrying out its operations satisfactorily. Emails would pass through their servers, located in Fairfax, Virginia, with impressive speed, to the point that the company often received compliments from its users. Then, one day, the CauliflowerOnline servers slowed tremendously. The company’s logs showed that emails had been transmitted en mass to nearly fifteen thousand of the company’s account-holders. This of course alarmed the company’s management; with the CauliflowerOnline being as small as it is, the wave of emails was unprecedented in the company’s history. Though the company’s leaders at first decided to just take this hit, assuming that it would not happen again, they were shocked when another twelve thousand emails passed through their server eight days later, another nineteen thousand emails only three days after that, and a final nine thousand the following day. By that time, they had determined by checking the server logs that the emails had all originated from a man named Jeremy Reynolds, who had sent the emails on behalf of GoodIntentions of Greater Washington, a charity organization.

Pro-Plaintiff Knowledge:

CauliflowerOnline’s servers are located in Fairfax, Virginia. Through CauliflowerOnline’s servers, Jeremy Reynolds sent, on multiple occasions, masses of more than ten thousand emails within a one-day period. The emails which Reynolds distributed featured advertised the sale of merchandise.

Pro-Defendant Knowledge:

Though so many emails passed through CauliflowerOnline’s servers all at once, the company’s servers are incredibly robust, and so their regular operation was impacted only slightly. For this reason, the CauliflowerOnline received only a handful of complaints from its users. In addition, the emails were meant to benefit GoodIntentions, which is a charitable organization.


Defendant Witness: Jeremy Reynolds

Jeremy Reynolds is a member of GoodIntentions of Greater Washington. GoodIntentions is a non-profit organization known for retail stores across the D.C. metropolitan area. Jeremy Reynolds has been a renowned member of GoodIntentions charity for a long time. Jeremy Reynolds has a good history of being a charitable member of GoodIntention and his work for the better future of the impoverished has also been appreciated by many.

Jeremy Reynolds has been looking over to expand the charity reach and also gather more and more charity for the impoverished who are benefited by GoodIntentions. To get greater attention to the GoodIntentions and how the charity is being done for the impoverished by the GoodIntentions, Jeremy Reynolds decided to send out emails. Reynolds gathered the emails of users of CauliflowerOnline email service, knowing that CauliflowerOnline has a reliable server which has users who might be willing to help the good cause. To spread the history of GoodIntentions and how the organization achieves their mission, Reynolds sent out over 12,000 unsolicited emails to users of CauliflowerOnline on 4 separate occasions. These emails contained an overview of work done by the GoodIntentions and how the charity works. The email also contained the advertisement of the merchandise and the stores which help the charity done by GoodIntentions. The emails contained the information that when a merchandise is being purchased from the mentioned retail stores, the profits are donated to the charity which supports GoodIntentions. Reynolds was sending out these emails, thinking that it was protected speech as the emails were helping the charity of GoodIntentions and did not support major commercial advertisement.

Knowing that some users of CauliflowerOnline might not be interested in receiving any further emails about GoodIntention and its charity, Reynolds also included an “opt out” option in the emails so that the users can avoid receiving any more emails from GoodIntention. This option was introduced to provide convenience to any users who would not like any more emails from GoodIntentions.

Pro-Plaintiff Knowledge:

The email addresses of the users of CauliflowerOnline were obtained by Jeremy Reynolds from the business partners of GoodIntentions. The emails were indeed non-commercial in nature but did violate the spam laws as they still appeared to be unsolicited. The emails sent to the users of CauliflowerOnline contained merchandise which in a sense advertised the merchandise more than the message of the good cause by GoodIntentions charity.

Pro-Defendant Knowledge:

Jeremy Reynolds had no intention to spread the commercial nature of their charity, the emails were only to spread the charity done by GoodIntentions. The emails sent through the server were divided into parts to avoid load on the server of CauliflowerOnline. The emails sent to the users by Jeremy Reynolds also had an option to “opt out” which upon selecting would let users not receive any further emails from GoodIntentions charity.


Defendant Witness: GoodIntentions charity Representative

GoodIntentions Charity is a unique charity that aims to help the impoverished through the sale of our merchandise in our retail stores. We opened our stores for the sole purpose of helping people and families throughout the area. We do not operate to make a profit. That is why we are registered as a non-profit organization. We as a group only have good intentions in whatever action that we take. It was with that idea that we called ourselves the GoodIntentions charity.

The main goal of GoodIntentions was to create awareness of our existence. Since everyone has an email account these we decided that sending people emails about our mission and our methods would bring about a greater awareness in people of what we do. One of our members Jeremy Reynolds was astute enough to notice that the only way to help more people was to reach out to people and let them know of our purpose. He decided that sending emails with our motivations and purpose with a few merchandise was the best way to reach out to new people. Through a business partner he was able to obtain mailing lists of people from CauliflowerOnline among other email services. It was a fully legal approach to expanding our network of potential customers, who are also donors when they make purchases at GoodIntentions.

The focus of the emails were the message to help those who were impoverished. That was the only goal. There was of course some merchandise in the emails but that was just an aside on the overall plan. They were there solely so that if the recipients felt compelled enough to donate after reading our cause then they could choose to do so. Jeremy having the technical knowledge about these things decided to send mass emails to people in hopes to get more donations. He had only good intentions in mind while he did this. He reasoned that the more emails we send the more people will be compelled to look at our message. There was no hidden motivation to gain money. As we have said before, we are a non-profit organization. Profit is the last thing we are after. We only want to help those in need.

Pro-Plaintiff Knowledge:

The emails contained merchandise that were available for sale. One of the side goals was to get money from the email recipients as well. The placement of merchandise was intentional.

Pro-Defendant Knowledge:

The emails did not cause a significant lag which resulted in minimal complaints from the users. The email list was obtained legally. The emails were sent periodically so that the servers would not get overloaded.
